Methods

This page documents how data is structured, displayed, and analyzed in the Alaska Landslide Inventory viewer. It covers the classification system, how map features are positioned, how areas and volumes are measured, and how the two timeline charts work.

1. Data sources and structure

The inventory contains two populations of landslides, stored in separate GeoPackage files and merged into a single PostGIS database on load:

  • Slow landslides (~1,110 records) — attributes and geometry co-located in a single GeoPackage layer. Each record has one polygon representing the landslide body.
  • Catastrophic landslides (~314 records, ~621 polygons) — attributes in one GeoPackage, polygons in a separate GeoPackage, linked by the Unique name field. Polygons are tagged as either source (headscarp/failure area) or deposit (runout/debris field).

All source geometry is in Alaska Albers Equal Area (EPSG:6393). During loading, geometry is reprojected to WGS 84 (EPSG:4326) for web display. Area measurements are preserved from the original equal-area projection and are not recomputed in WGS 84.

2. Classification system

Every landslide has a type (slow or catastrophic) and a class that encodes both the type and the strength of evidence for ongoing creep. Classes are organized in the sidebar from most to least active:

Slow landslides

Slow Subtle creep Yellow Creep detectable by InSAR or careful image comparison, not visually obvious

On the map, catastrophic landslides with precursory creep are drawn with a colored halo (stroke) matching their creep evidence level, making them visually distinct from catastrophic events without known precursory deformation.

3. Polygons and map placement

Polygon roles

Each polygon in the database has a role:

  • body — the single polygon for a slow landslide, representing its full mapped extent.
  • source — the headscarp or failure initiation zone of a catastrophic event.
  • deposit — the runout or debris deposit of a catastrophic event.

A typical catastrophic landslide has one source polygon and one deposit polygon. Some have only a deposit (the source may be obscured or uncertain). The is_primary flag disambiguates in rare cases where multiple polygons share a role.

Centroid placement

The map dot for each landslide is placed at the centroid of a single representative polygon, chosen by priority:

  1. For slow landslides: centroid of the body polygon.
  2. For catastrophic landslides: centroid of the primary source polygon. If no source polygon exists, the primary deposit polygon is used as a fallback.

This means the dot marks where the failure initiated, not where debris came to rest. Polygons themselves appear when zoomed in past zoom level 7.

4. Areas and volumes

Areas

Polygon areas are stored from the original GeoPackage data, which was digitized and measured in Alaska Albers Equal Area (EPSG:6393). This is an equal-area projection, so area measurements are geometrically correct. Areas are not recomputed from the WGS 84 coordinates stored in the database (which would introduce substantial error at Alaska latitudes).

The “source area” filter uses:

  • For slow landslides: the body polygon area (the entire landslide).
  • For catastrophic landslides: the source polygon area.

The “deposit area” filter applies only to catastrophic landslides. Slow landslides pass through this filter unconditionally (they have no deposit).

Volumes

Preferred volume estimates (volume_preferred) are taken directly from the source data. The estimation method varies by landslide and is recorded in the volume_method field, visible in the detail panel.

Area and volume sliders use a logarithmic scale:

  • Area: slider range 0–6 maps to 103–109 m² (1,000 m² to 1,000 km²).
  • Volume: slider range 0–8 maps to 104–1012 m³ (10,000 m³ to 1 km³).

Landslides with no area or volume data always pass these filters (they are not excluded by a minimum threshold for a measurement that was never made).

5. Temporal data and year encoding

Temporal precision varies enormously across the inventory. The system encodes each landslide’s age as a year number using this priority:

  1. Class contains “Holocene” → year = −1 (prehistoric, roughly 10,000+ years ago)
  2. Class contains “Modern” → year = 0 (post-settlement but pre-2000, roughly 150 years)
  3. year_text is a four-digit year → that year (e.g., 2013)
  4. date_min exists → year extracted from it

The Min age slider maps positions to this encoding: position 0 = all, 1 = Holocene+, 2 = Modern+, 3–16 = 2012–2025. This provides a coarse temporal filter that works despite the mix of precise dates and order-of-magnitude age estimates.

Precise dates

Catastrophic landslides may have:

  • Seismic datetime — precise to the second, from seismic detection (stored as UTC).
  • Date range (date_min / date_max) — a bracket: the event occurred sometime between these dates. The span can be hours (for well-observed events) to months or years.

The “Time bracketed” include-only checkbox restricts to landslides that have both date_min and date_max. The “Seismic detection” checkbox restricts to landslides with a seismic datetime.

6. Seasonal timing chart

The seasonal histogram shows when in the year catastrophic events occur. Only landslides with seismic datetimes or valid date brackets are included.

Binning

The year is divided into 37 bins: 36 bins of 10 days each (days 1–10, 11–20, …, 351–360) plus one shorter bin for the remaining 5–6 days. The final bin’s bar height is normalized to a 10-day equivalent so it is visually comparable.

Weight distribution

  • Point events (seismic datetime): full weight of 1.0 assigned to the bin containing that day of year.
  • Range events (date bracket): weight is spread proportionally across all bins the range overlaps. Each bin receives weight = (days of overlap with bin) / (total span in days). This means a 30-day bracket contributes 1.0 total, distributed across the ~3 bins it touches, rather than counting once in each bin.

Events spanning the year boundary (December into January) are handled correctly by splitting the range into two sub-ranges.

Max uncertainty slider

The “Max uncertainty” slider (default: 30 days) excludes range events with spans longer than the slider value. This filters out events whose timing is too uncertain to contribute meaningful seasonal information. Setting it to 0 shows only seismically detected events.

Y-axis units

The y-axis shows events per 10-day bin, not raw counts. Because range events distribute fractional weight, non-integer values are expected.

7. Timeline chart

The timeline histogram shows when in history landslides occurred. All landslides with resolvable temporal data are included (not just time-bracketed events).

Bin structure

The chart uses variable-width bins that reflect increasing data completeness in recent periods:

PeriodBin widthVisual width
Holocene (year = −1) ~10,000 years 27 units
Modern pre-2000 (year = 0) ~150 years 45 units
Annual 2000–2011 1 year each 9 units each
Monthly 2012–present 1 month each 1 unit each

The 2012+ zone is highlighted with a blue background, indicating the period of most complete satellite coverage and systematic inventory.

Rate normalization

Raw counts are converted to a common unit of events per year to allow comparison across vastly different bin widths:

  • Holocene count ÷ 10,000
  • Modern count ÷ 150
  • Annual bins (2000–2011) are already per-year
  • Monthly bins × 12 to annualize

The divisors for Holocene and Modern are approximate. The true Holocene spans ~11,700 years; the “Modern” category encompasses events of varying age. These are order-of-magnitude normalizations for visual comparison, not precise rate estimates. The annualization of monthly bins treats all months as 1/12 of a year regardless of actual length.

Monthly spreading (2012+)

For the monthly-resolution portion of the timeline, events are placed as follows:

  • Seismic point: assigned entirely to the month of the seismic detection.
  • Date range: weight is spread proportionally across months based on the fraction of the range falling in each month.
  • Year only (no date range): spread equally across all 12 months of that year (1/12 each).

Dashed horizontal lines show the annual average for each post-2012 year.

Log scale

A “log” checkbox switches the y-axis to a logarithmic scale (base-10). This is useful because Holocene and Modern rates are typically orders of magnitude lower than modern detection rates, and the linear scale compresses them to near-zero.

8. Filter mechanics

All filtering is performed client-side on the full dataset. The map and both charts respond to the same set of filters:

Type and class checkboxes

Type (Slow / Catastrophic) and class checkboxes are combined with AND logic: a landslide must match at least one checked type AND at least one checked class.

Include-only checkboxes

When checked, these restrict the display to landslides having a specific attribute (e.g., molards, dammed streams, seismic detection). Multiple checked boxes are AND’d: the landslide must satisfy all of them.

Area, volume, and age sliders

These apply a minimum threshold. All use ≥ (greater-than-or-equal) comparison. Landslides with missing data for the filtered attribute pass through unconditionally — the filter only excludes records where the value is known and below the threshold.

Viewport limiting

Both charts always restrict to the current map viewport. The sidebar counts can optionally be restricted to the viewport via the “Limit counts to map view” checkbox.

URL state

All filter settings, slider positions, and panel visibility are encoded in the URL query string. This means filtered views can be shared as links that reproduce the exact same view.

9. Known limitations

  • Completeness varies by era. The inventory is most complete for the post-2012 satellite era. Holocene and Modern event counts are severe underestimates of actual occurrence rates. The timeline normalization makes this visually apparent but the magnitudes should not be taken as true rates.
  • Temporal precision varies widely. Some events are timed to the second (seismic); others have year or century-scale uncertainty. The seasonal histogram’s “max uncertainty” slider helps manage this, but users should be aware that the seasonal distribution is dominated by well-timed events.
  • Volume estimates use varied methods. The volume_method field in the detail panel describes how each estimate was derived. Comparisons across landslides with different methods should be made cautiously.
  • “Source area” label for slow landslides. The “Min source area” slider filters on the body polygon area for slow landslides (since they have no separate source/deposit distinction). This is the total mapped extent of the slow landslide.
  • Monthly rate annualization. Monthly event counts are multiplied by 12 to produce an annualized rate. This treats all months as equal length, which introduces a small systematic bias (up to ~10% for February vs. months with 31 days).
  • Holocene and Modern rate divisors. The Holocene rate uses a 10,000-year divisor and the Modern rate uses 150 years. These are reasonable approximations but are not tied to precise epoch boundaries.
